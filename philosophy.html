How I think about it

My Philosophy

On commitment, purpose, and the ethics questions that come up constantly when you're doing community health work.

Purpose

On dedication and the long game

Medicine takes a long time. The path from where I am — pre-med at UCSD — to attending physician in pediatric hematology/oncology is roughly twelve years. A lot of people treat that timeline as a waiting room. I don't.

What you do before medical school isn't just preparation. It's where you decide whether you actually have something to offer, what problems you're capable of sitting with, and whether the work you gravitate toward makes someone's situation concretely better or just makes you feel productive. Those are different things, and the gap between them is bigger than most people admit.

My framework is simple: build things that outlast your involvement, work on problems where failure is instructive and success is visible, and stay close to patients and communities even when you're technically a student. The navigator work and the research are both ways of refusing to wait. You can do real work before you have an MD, if you're honest about your scope and disciplined about staying in it.

Why pediatrics

Pediatrics isn't just a specialty preference. It's a belief that early-life health disparities compound in ways that make intervention at this stage more consequential than at almost any other point. A child who gets to the follow-up appointment, whose family has food security and stable housing, who grows up with functional access to care — that's a different adult and a different life trajectory. The leverage is real.

Pediatric hematology/oncology specifically because the cases are medically complex, the stakes are high, and the patients can't fully advocate for themselves. I want to be in rooms where the decisions are hard and the reasoning matters.

Bioethics

Where I'm going intellectually

My formal ethics work is early. Through SDIPP I've built ethics frameworks for community health volunteer programs — working through consent design, scope of practice limits, and referral obligations in settings where the standard answers don't quite fit. That work has pushed me toward questions I want to pursue more rigorously.

Two areas in particular.

On rationing

Healthcare rationing is usually framed as an emergency problem — the ventilator shortage, the organ transplant list. But it's a constant reality in community health. Which family gets the navigator this week? Which referral gets prioritized when a volunteer has four hours? Which SDOH barrier gets addressed first when a family has five?

The frameworks matter. Whether you prioritize the worst off, maximize aggregate benefit, or try to correct for structural disadvantage shapes real decisions — not just policy positions. The arguments around pediatric care are especially interesting because children can't advocate for themselves in the standard way, and because early-life disparities compound over time in ways that make priority arguments substantially stronger than they'd be for the same condition in an adult.

The fair innings argument, the rule of rescue, the tension between efficiency and equity — these aren't abstract. They come up every time you have a finite resource and multiple people who need it. I want to understand them well enough to make defensible decisions, not just go with instinct.

On paternalism

The spectrum runs from soft paternalism — ensure genuinely informed consent, then respect the choice — to libertarian paternalism, where you design the environment so the safe option is easiest without restricting alternatives, to hard paternalism, which overrides choice regardless of understanding.

Soft paternalism is the operating standard most of the time. But in community health settings, "genuinely informed consent" is much harder to achieve than the phrase implies. A family that doesn't trust the healthcare system, can't access information in their primary language, has been failed by referrals that went nowhere, and is making decisions under significant financial and logistical pressure — that family isn't making a free choice when they decline follow-up care. Respecting autonomy in that context requires more than handing them a form.

Libertarian paternalism — nudge design — is underused in community health. Making the safe behavior the easy default, without closing off the alternatives, is ethically defensible and practically effective. It's the right design principle for most of the programs I work on. Where it becomes interesting is the edge cases: when does designing an environment to make a certain choice easy slide into manipulation? What does the family have a right to know about how the choice architecture was built?

I don't have clean answers to these. That's partly why I'm interested in them.

The ethics interest isn't separate from the clinical work — it's generated by it. The rationing questions come from navigation work where there are more families than hours. The paternalism questions come from designing SDIPP programs where the right amount of structure isn't obvious. I'm looking for formal research involvement that connects empirical community health work with these harder philosophical questions.
