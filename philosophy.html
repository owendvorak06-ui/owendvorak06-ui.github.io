How I think about it

My Philosophy

On commitment, purpose, and the ethics questions that come up constantly when you're doing community health work.

Purpose

On dedication and the long game

Medicine takes a long time. The path from where I am, pre-med at UCSD, to attending physician in pediatric hematology/oncology is roughly twelve years. A lot of people treat that timeline as a waiting room. I don't.

What you do before medical school isn't just preparation. It's where you decide whether you actually have something to offer, what problems you're capable of sitting with, and whether the work you gravitate toward makes someone's situation concretely better or just makes you feel productive. Those are different things, and the gap between them is bigger than most people admit.

My framework is simple: build things that outlast your involvement, work on problems where failure is instructive and success is visible, and stay close to patients and communities even when you're technically a student. The navigator work and the research are both ways of refusing to wait. You can do real work before you have an MD, if you're honest about your scope and disciplined about staying in it.

Why pediatrics

Pediatrics isn't just a specialty preference. It's a belief that early-life health disparities compound in ways that make intervention at this stage more consequential than at almost any other point. A child who gets to the follow-up appointment, whose family has food security and stable housing, who grows up with functional access to care: that's a different adult and a different life trajectory.

Pediatric hematology/oncology specifically because the cases are medically complex, the stakes are high, and the patients can't fully advocate for themselves. I want to be in rooms where the decisions are hard and the reasoning matters.

Bioethics

Where I'm going intellectually

My formal ethics work is early. Through SDIPP I've built ethics frameworks for community health volunteer programs, working through consent design, scope of practice limits, and referral obligations in settings where the standard answers don't quite fit. That work has pushed me toward questions I want to pursue more rigorously.

Two areas in particular.

On rationing

Healthcare rationing is usually framed as an emergency problem: the ventilator shortage, the organ transplant list. But it's a constant reality in community health. Which family gets the navigator this week? Which referral gets prioritized when a volunteer has four hours? Which SDOH barrier gets addressed first when a family has five?

The framework you use isn't neutral. Prioritizing the worst-off leads to different decisions than maximizing aggregate benefit or trying to correct for structural disadvantage. And the arguments shift in pediatric care because children can't advocate for themselves in the standard way, and because early-life disparities compound over time. The case for prioritizing a disadvantaged child is stronger than the case for prioritizing a disadvantaged adult in otherwise identical circumstances.

The fair innings argument, the rule of rescue, the tension between efficiency and equity: these aren't abstract positions. They come up every time you have a finite resource and multiple people who need it. I want to understand them well enough to make defensible decisions.

On paternalism

Hard paternalism gets a bad reputation in bioethics. The standard position is soft paternalism: ensure genuinely informed consent, then respect whatever the person chooses. Libertarian paternalism is the popular compromise; nudge architecture that makes the safe option easier without restricting alternatives. Both treat autonomy as nearly inviolable.

I'm more sympathetic to Sarah Conly's position in Against Autonomy. Her argument is that cognitive biases are systematic and predictable, that people frequently choose against their own stated long-term goals, and that overriding those choices in high-stakes domains is not only permissible but sometimes required. I think she's right. And I think the medical community is far too cautious about acting on it.

The clearest example is high fructose corn syrup. The causal links to obesity, type 2 diabetes, and their downstream conditions are well-documented. The individual harms compound over years in ways that make meaningful consent difficult; no one at 15 really understands what metabolic disease at 45 looks like. If a medical case for banning HFCS were presented to a legislature, the correct bioethical position is not to object on autonomy grounds. It's to support the ban.

This extends to clinical practice. The deference to patient preference over clinical judgment has costs that medicine systematically underestimates. A family that declines follow-up care is not exercising autonomous agency in any robust sense when their choice is shaped by insurance confusion, transportation barriers, distrust from prior failures, and the cognitive load of several other concurrent crises. Calling that "respecting autonomy" flatters the clinician while abandoning the patient.

Where the argument weakens is at the edge cases. Conly's framework requires a sufficiently clear picture of what the person's long-term interests actually are. When values rather than biases drive the divergence, hard paternalism becomes harder to justify. I don't have a clean line there. That's part of why I want to work through it more rigorously.

The ethics interest isn't separate from the clinical work; it's generated by it. The rationing questions come from navigation work where there are more families than hours. The paternalism questions come from designing SDIPP programs where the right amount of structure isn't obvious. I'm looking for formal research involvement that connects empirical community health work with these harder philosophical questions.
