About

I'm a pre-medical student at UC San Diego, focused on pediatric medicine and community health. Most of my work centers on the gap between clinical care and what actually happens when a family leaves the hospital: whether they get to the follow-up appointment, whether anyone screens them for the barriers that make that impossible, whether a student with training and time can close that gap.

I lead the San Diego Injury Prevention Program, a student-run nonprofit operating in senior residential facilities, schools, and community health fairs across San Diego. I also founded the Aristeia Health Initiative, where I'm developing student-navigator programs embedded in pediatric clinical settings at Rady Children's Hospital. Alongside both, I operate a direct patient navigation service for families leaving Rady: I contact them post-discharge, screen for social determinants barriers, and work through whatever blocks them from getting to care.

I want to go into pediatric hematology/oncology. Before that, I'm spending these years building programs that give other pre-med students a real, patient-benefit role in clinical settings — and learning what community health ethics looks like in the spaces hospitals don't cover.

Patient Navigator Service

Aristeia Health Initiative  ·  Independent

I operate an independent patient navigation service for pediatric families at discharge from Rady Children's Hospital. Working from referrals from the Rady social work team, I contact families within 48–72 hours of discharge and run a full social determinants intake: transportation access, insurance status, housing stability, food security, language, and childcare. I stay on the case, week over week, until every follow-up appointment is scheduled and confirmed.

  • Full SDOH screen at intake: transport, insurance, housing, food, language, childcare
  • Insurance verification and Medi-Cal reinstatement navigation
  • Weekly check-ins until case closure; post-appointment follow-through within 48 hours
  • Escalation protocols for mental health crises, safety concerns, and missed appointments
  • Case documentation in a structured tracking database for every contact attempt and outcome

Building toward a formal AHI volunteer navigator program with IRB-approved outcomes data collection and a Rady partnership agreement.

Aristeia Health Initiative

Pediatric community health, San Diego

AHI is a student-run health research and community intervention organization focused on pediatric community health in San Diego. Five projects are in active development, each built around a trained student navigator embedded in a clinical or community setting. The goal is peer-reviewed publication alongside direct patient benefit.

  • LGBTQ Youth Healthcare Navigation: navigation toolkit and workshops; primary partner: The LGBTQ Center San Diego
  • Pediatric Chronic Disease Navigator: SDOH screening and resource referral embedded in a Rady specialty clinic (sickle cell, asthma)
  • Mental Health Referral Follow-Through: post-referral support for families who never connect with mental health services after a primary care referral
  • Transition to Adult Care Navigator: youth with chronic conditions at the pediatric-to-adult care transition (sickle cell, T1D, IBD)
  • Hem/Onc and Social Determinants: SDOH screening embedded in the Rady hem/onc clinic, paired with a student navigator clinical role

Target journals: Academic Pediatrics, Journal of Community Health, Pediatrics, Injury Prevention.

What I'm Building

Most pre-med volunteer work is passive: sit in clinic, observe, check a box. What AHI is trying to build is different. The model puts trained student navigators into clinical settings with a real scope of work, doing work that directly determines whether a family gets to care.

A navigator contacts the family within 48 hours of discharge or referral. They run a structured SDOH screen — not a checklist but a real conversation — and find the actual barriers: a bus ride that takes two hours each way, an insurance card that lapsed because the annual renewal notice went to an old address, a fear of the clinic that nobody addressed. They solve problems on the phone. They arrange NEMT rides, call providers, sit on hold, send texts the night before appointments. They follow up after the visit. They keep the case open until everything is done.

Students in this role develop skills nobody teaches in pre-med coursework: how to talk to a family that is overwhelmed and skeptical, how to work Medi-Cal, what the actual gap between a referral and a completed appointment looks like, what "social determinants of health" means outside a lecture slide. The program generates outcomes data — appointment adherence rates, barrier patterns, case closure timelines — that can be published and used to scale the model to other institutions.

This is also the kind of work that shapes how you practice medicine. Understanding why patients don't show up, what it costs them to try, and what it takes to actually help is not something you learn from a textbook. It's learned by being on the phone with a family trying to figure out how to get their child to a neurology appointment in a neighborhood three bus transfers away.

Bioethics

My formal work in bioethics is early. Through SDIPP, I've built ethics frameworks for community health volunteer programs — working through consent design, scope of practice limits, and referral obligations in settings where the standard textbook answers don't quite fit. That work has led me toward questions I want to pursue more rigorously.

What draws me most: the ethics of patient autonomy when structural barriers make genuine informed choice more complicated than the standard framework assumes. How do you honor autonomous decision-making for a family that doesn't trust the healthcare system, can't easily access care, or has been failed repeatedly by referrals that went nowhere? The patient navigation work surfaces these questions constantly. It's not an abstract problem — it comes up in every intake call.

The standard four-principle framework (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) starts to strain when you're working with families who face structural barriers to exercising any of those principles meaningfully. The question I keep returning to is what "respecting autonomy" actually requires structurally, not just procedurally. The gap between a signed consent form and a family that genuinely understands their options and can act on them is enormous in community health settings.

I'm developing a working relationship with Reuven Brandt at UCSD's Philosophy department, who works in applied ethics and epistemic autonomy. I want to connect the empirical patterns I'm seeing in community health work with the harder philosophical questions about disclosure, obligation, and what meaningful autonomy requires at the structural level.

I'm looking for formal ethics research or education involvement: QI projects, clinical ethics consultation research, curriculum development, or faculty mentorship in applied bioethics. If there's a fit, I'd like to hear about it.
