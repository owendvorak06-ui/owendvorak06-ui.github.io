For Students

Some advice on meaningful community health work, and how to get involved with AHI and SDIPP in San Diego.

The honest take

Most pre-med volunteering is a waste of time

Not the time you spend. The potential of what that time could be. Sitting in a waiting room, shadowing a physician who doesn't know your name, distributing pamphlets at a health fair: these activities are fine, but they don't teach you anything you couldn't have learned another way, and they don't make anyone's situation concretely better.

The gap between "doing a thing" and "doing a thing that actually matters" is something most pre-med programs don't address. Admissions offices say they want meaningful experience. What they usually get is documented hours.

What makes experience actually meaningful isn't the setting. It's whether someone needed you to do what you did. Whether your presence and effort changed what happened for a patient or a family. Whether you learned something that couldn't have been learned sitting in a library.

The standard

What meaningful work actually looks like

You have a defined scope
You know exactly what you're responsible for, what's in your scope to handle, and when to escalate. You're trained, deployed, and accountable.
Your absence has consequences
If you don't show up, something doesn't get done. A family doesn't get called. An appointment doesn't get scheduled. A class doesn't happen. The work is real enough to create a gap.
You're learning things school won't teach
How to talk to a family who is overwhelmed and skeptical. How Medi-Cal actually works. What "social determinants" look like when you're on the phone with someone trying to figure out a bus route to a specialist three cities over.
It compounds
The cases and programs you work on generate outcomes data, professional relationships, and institutional knowledge that builds over time. You're contributing to something that gets better as you put more into it.

Get involved

The navigator role at AHI

AHI is building student-navigator positions embedded in clinical settings at Rady Children's Hospital. A navigator contacts families post-discharge or post-referral, runs a structured SDOH screen, and works cases until every follow-up appointment is closed. It's hands-on, patient-facing, and skills-intensive in ways that most pre-med volunteer roles aren't.

What you'll actually do: make calls, verify insurance, arrange NEMT rides, sit on hold with provider offices, send reminders, follow up after appointments, escalate when something looks serious. You'll learn Medi-Cal navigation, SDOH barrier patterns, how the referral-to-appointment pipeline actually works, and how to have real conversations with families under stress.

What I'm looking for in navigators: reliability first, then genuine curiosity about the community health problems, then the ability to think on your feet when a call doesn't go the way the protocol describes.

The navigator training is structured: you learn the intake protocol, the SDOH screening questions, the escalation decision trees, and the insurance verification steps before you take a live case. You're not thrown in cold.

Also worth knowing about

SDIPP is different and both are worth your time

SDIPP runs hands-on injury prevention programs across San Diego: weekly mobility classes at senior residential facilities, health literacy programs in schools, community health fairs, and project-based work like sidewalk safety. The volunteer structure is flexible; most programs run on weekends, no prerequisites, no minimum commitment.

SDIPP and AHI are different kinds of experience. AHI is more intensive: individual case management, direct family contact, complex navigation. SDIPP is community-embedded programming at scale, where you're working directly with participants in a group or community setting. Both are worth doing. They build different skills.
